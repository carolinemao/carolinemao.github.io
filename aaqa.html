Overview

The Asian Academic Quiz Alliance (AAQA) is an organization which aims to promote the academic competition of quizbowl in East and Southeast Asia. Established in spring 2020, AAQA hosts quizbowl tournaments and conducts outreach to high schools.

As the UI/UX designer, my goal was to design a website which builds the Asian quizbowl community. Few organizations have existed specifically to support Asian quizbowl, as quizbowl is mostly centered in the USA, so this was an opportunity to create and strengthen connections between Asian quizbowlers. I also needed to keep in mind the challenges of the COVID-19 pandemic, which caused quizbowl to shift to online tournaments for the 2020-21 school year.

Priorities

The AAQA committee provided me with an initial list of pages they wanted for the website: about us, introduction to quizbowl, blog, tournaments, championship, and the rules. I also added a contact page to the list of tasks.

I narrowed this down to the few most important pages as my areas of focus:

  • Tournaments: Tournaments are the main event in quizbowl, a nd the most important for teams to find. I also needed to consider the unique challenges of tournaments being mostly online due to COVID-19, and how I could make them more accessible in a time where in-person outreach/recruitment is difficult.
  • Blog: Blog articles help engage users further and encourage them to return to the website for updates. They’re also an easy way to provide informational resources on what quizbowl is and how it works, enabling outreach.
  • Homepage: Centralizing the website and vital to inspiring users to look around the website, return, and further their interest in quizbowl.
The other pages were also important, but required few complex interactions and were largely static. The introduction to quizbowl, championship, and rules pages I assumed would look similar to blog posts, so they weren’t as high on the priority list.

Initial wireframes

Color and font swatches

Colors: I wanted to draw from the site’s Asian associations and began with bright shades of red and peach, but realized that they were too alarming by themselves, so I added purple as the main color to stabilize it. Though vibrant and appealing, the analogous reds and purples don’t contrast too highly, creating a sense of peace.

Fonts: Drawing from the blogging site Medium, I combined a serif heading with a sans serif body. The contrast and font weights made the text easier to read and provided visual hierarchy.

Design system + components

Tournaments page

Wireframe

Lo-fi prototype

Challenges

  • How do I display information to the user in a way that’s readable and accessible?
  • What information does the user want to know most about a tournament?
  • How do I get people to add and host tournaments, encouraging regional growth?
  • How can I make it simplest for the user to find the specific tournaments they need?

Solutions

Display tournaments on cards

Used a simple card format to display tournament information users would find most important: name, host, date, location, competition difficulty, and question set. (Different tournaments sometimes use the same question set, so teams must avoid playing tournaments using the same set.)

Condense information on cards

Though event cards typically include images to increase visual appeal, upcoming quizbowl tournaments typically don’t have any associated images. I thus included only the most vital info on cards.

I considered minified cards which expanded on click, but deemed them unnecessary since the small size of the Asian quizbowl circuits leads to few tournaments each year, minimizing the amount of cards.

Calendar as a visual reference

A small calendar fixed to the side allows the user to immediately see tournaments happening this month. Provides a helpful reference; if they want more info, they can look at the tournament cards.

Organize tournaments with filters

A search bar and filters by regions, date range, question set, and difficulty level were included to help the user find tournaments suiting their needs. Stickied at the top to simplify navigation.

Encourage tournament hosting

Information can be found on the side on how to host a tournament and add it to the page, offering potential tournament hosts a resource and point of contact.

Blog page

Wireframe

Lo-fi prototypes

Challenges

  • How do I get users to look at the most important and interesting articles?
  • What is the most important information to display for each blog post, and why?
  • How do I allow the user to find blog posts tailored to their specific interests?
  • How can I keep the user engaged when faced with large amounts of text content?

Solutions

Prominently feature the most important articles

I explored several different ways of displaying featured articles in my wireframe and prototypes. I settled on a slider that displayed three posts at once, minimizing the amount of clicking through the slider the user needs to do, and included large images to maximize visual appeal.

Minimize the vertical size of the feature banner

While the banner allows users to get a glimpse into what the blog has to offer, it becomes irritating if the user needs to scroll too far to see the articles, which should be the main focus.

Sidebar filters for more specific user needs

The user is provided a searchbar for specific needs. The use of category tags and a popular posts widget also suggests ways the user can find posts they like without becoming overwhelmed with content. Fixed position for easy navigation; I discarded a lo-fi providing a searchbar in the header to eliminate the need for the user to scroll back up to it.

Promote engagement and contact with AAQA

The sidebar’s inclusion of social media links (also included in the footer) and an offer to subscribe to blog updates increases the user’s likeliness of maintaing consistent contact with the site and AAQA as an organization.

Homepage

Wireframe & lo-fi

Challenges

  • How do I divide up information on the homepage to avoid overwhelming the user with content?
  • How do I make the homepage sufficiently interesting and engaging to users, especially first-time users unfamiliar with the rest of the site?
  • What information does the user most want to see when they first arrive on this website?

Solutions

Divide page into sections and establish a hierarchy

Background colors and varying visual styles separate each block of information. As the mainstay events of quizbowl, tournaments were displayed first, immediately after the header. Blog articles went next since they provide the user with quizbowl resources, then information about the annual championship and links to other pages in AAQA.

Simple navigation

Initially, the committee provided me with more pages than I could link in a single line of the header, so I organized it further by linking secondary pages such as intro to quizbowl and championship information in a dropdown

Increasing visual appeal

Lots of images are included to increase visual delight for the user. Variation in each section, such as by alternating between one-column and multi-column formats, break up the monotony of the page.

Minimize unimportant information

To reduce the amount of vertical scrolling and cognitive load on the user, each section includes prominent headings and only a select few pieces of important information from their respective pages. COA buttons at the end of each section offer an opportunity to learn more.

Reflections

This was my first project working as a solo designer+developer, so it was a really interesting challenge I learned a lot from. I was also really excited to combine my passion for design with my love of quizbowl, an extracurricular I’ve participated in since high school.

Communication and initiative: I checked in with the AAQA committee frequently so they could approve designs and provide feedback throughout the design process. However, I needed to be highly independent and take initiative in setting my own deadlines and creating designs even when given minimal feedback.

Short timeline: Quizbowl is an academic activity which happens mainly during the school year, so I wanted to get the site up as soon as possible It was vital to get designs and development worked out as quickly as possible, reducing the amount of time I’d like to ideate and iterate.

Lack of a developer or designer team: As a computer science major with front-end development experience, I was excited to get the chance to use my development skills. However, managing as a solo designer/developer meant user research will be vital to receive feedback, and I made most decisions by myself.

Promoting quizbowl: Local quizbowl organizations in the past have often had difficult-to-navigate websites which quickly fell out of date, hindering outreach. I strove to design a website that encouraged outreach with helpful resources and tournament details, while not taking much effort for our webmasters to maintain.
